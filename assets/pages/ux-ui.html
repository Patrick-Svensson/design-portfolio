DramScape An online whisky shop that guides whisky enthusiasts to new whisky favourites.

I created this design during a period of 9 weeks for a UX project in the Udacity Nanodegree Program, using tools such as Miro, Figma, Zeplin, Photoshop, Lookback.

The Design Problem

The world of whisky/whiskey is vast. The beverage is being produced in many countries and from a variety of raw materials. Finding the way through that maze of whisky flavours can be difficult for users who are curious and seek new whisky experiences.

Since most local wine and spirit shops would have limited assortments, I aimed to find a solution to this problem by designing a web application that makes it easier for whisky enthusiasts (of drinking age, of course) to get introduced to exciting and delicious whiskies.

Research & Analysis

The semi-structured interviews revealed a couple of key insights that became important to the design process. 60% of the participants wanted the product information displayed to them in segments, with only basic information at the first presentation. Then, access further information by navigating to another page. 100% of the participants let the ratings given by other users guide them to what product to buy.

The synthesis of the research data from these insights gave me good clues to design the product presentation in a way that is efficient and useful to the user. The primary product presentation should consist of basic information only, such as brand name, type, volume, price, and user ratings.

Concepts & Sketching

In the empathise phase, I conducted semi-structured interviews with five whisky enthusiasts that fit the key profile. I organised the interview notes on an affinity board and then grouped them under different rubrics of Themes & Opportunities. The syntheses of that data made it possible for me to define the design problem as:

How might we provide information and guidance to whisky enthusiasts to find new whisky favourites?

During the ideation stage, I used techniques like Rapid Sketching and Crazy8 to come up with creative ideas for solutions to the design problem.

Several ideas were extracted from the sketches and written down on sticky notes. The ideas could then be sorted and prioritized with Idea Mapping and Value vs Complexity Quadrant.

By the end of the first iteration of the ideation stage, I had three solid ideas to bring forward into the phase of creating a low fidelity prototype of a specific user flow in the application.

  1. Include as much information as possible and display it in segments, prioritizing image, user ratings, and price.
  2. Place the preference filtering feature in the menu and provide the possibility to save the settings.
  3. Offer the users to buy sample vials of the products they find interesting.

Lo-Fi Prototyping

In Figma, I created low fidelity mockups and a prototype of the user flow for the whisky shop application. I find Figma an excellent tool to easily create and maneuver components and elements when designing mockups.

The mockups and prototype was designed for the following user flow:

  1. Navigate to the New In Stock page.
  2. Scroll and select a product on the New In Stock page.
  3. Enter the Product Details page by tapping the More Info button.
  4. Add a Sample Vial by tapping the cart button.
  5. Tap the X to close the pop-up box to return to New In Stock page (cart updated).

Testing

Most test recruits found the design easy and logical to use and efficiently managed to accomplish the flow task. There were however a couple of issues that needed attention.

  1. It was not obvious to some participants that the product page was scrollable.
  2. Some users did not primarily close the pop-up box by tapping on the exit-icon, but are used to the possibility of clicking anywhere on the screen window.

Design Iteration

To remedy the first problem and clarify the scrolling ability of the design, I opted for higher contrast between the product cards and the background to make the cards more conspicuous.

The second problem is probably more of a coding issue, but, to improve the prototype, I implemented the possibility to close the pop-up message box by tapping on the screen window.
